01 — Ontario's Hybrid Auto Insurance System

The foundation — understand this before anything else
🔵 No-Fault Side
  • Statutory Accident Benefits (SABs)
  • Claim from YOUR OWN insurer first
  • Fault is completely irrelevant
  • Designed for speed and certainty
  • Every injured person is compensated
🔴 Tort Side
  • Third Party Liability claims
  • Fault must be established
  • Sue for economic + pain & suffering damages
  • Contributory negligence applies
  • Governed by s.267.5 threshold
📌
Key framing: Ontario leans toward no-fault (more than most provinces, less than Quebec). All mandatory coverages are set by legislation — insurers cannot change the basic package under OAP 1.

02 — Statutory Accident Benefits (SABs)

No-fault benefits — respond first, regardless of who caused the accident
Eligibility Trigger
  • Injury from use or operation of a motor vehicle
  • Covers: driver · passenger · pedestrian · cyclist
  • Claim from own insurer first — fault irrelevant
Priority Rules — Who Pays?
1
Your own insurer
2
Spouse's insurer
3
Insurer of vehicle you were in
4
MVACF (last resort)
SAB Benefit Types
  • Medical / Rehab / Attendant Care
  • IRBs — 70% of gross income, weekly caps
  • Non-Earner Benefits (students/unemployed)
  • Caregiver Benefits
  • Death & Funeral Benefits
  • Optional: housekeeping, educational expenses
⚠️
Repeat emphasis from lecture (said 3 times): Fault does NOT matter for SABs. You receive benefits even if you caused the accident. This is the defining feature of no-fault benefits.

03 — Minor Injury Guideline (MIG)

O. Reg 34/10, s.3(1) — caps SAB treatment for soft tissue injuries
Injuries Within MIG
  • Sprains · Strains · Whiplash
  • Contusions · Abrasions · Lacerations
  • Sub-luxations
  • Full recovery typically expected
  • Strict cap on treatment funding
3 Ways to EXIT the MIG
1
Permanent spinal impairment
2
Significant loss of limb function
3
Chronic disabling pain
What Exiting MIG Unlocks
  • Higher SAB limits (non-catastrophic → higher caps)
  • Catastrophic = very high limits + lifetime care
  • Access to non-economic tort damages (pain & suffering)
  • MIG classification can change as injury evolves
🚨
Most common student error: MIG ONLY governs SAB (no-fault) benefit entitlement. It does NOT apply to tort-based claims. MIG ≠ s.267.5. They are similar concepts but legally distinct.

04 — DCPD & Uninsured Automobile Coverage

Two more mandatory buckets under OAP 1
DCPD — Direct Compensation Property Damage

Your insurer pays your vehicle damage when you're NOT at fault. All 4 must apply:

  • Accident occurred in Ontario
  • At least one other vehicle involved
  • Other vehicle insured by licensed Ontario insurer
  • You are not at fault under Fault Determination Rules
  • Your insurer then subrogate against the at-fault party
  • As of Jan 1, 2024 — drivers may opt out by signing a form
  • At-fault drivers: no DCPD — need optional collision cover
Uninsured Automobile Coverage (4th Mandatory Bucket)
  • Covers injury when at-fault driver has no insurance
  • Covers hit-and-run situations (unidentified driver)
  • Your own insurer steps in to provide coverage
Optional Add-Ons (not mandatory)
  • Enhanced accident benefits (higher limits)
  • Collision & Comprehensive (own vehicle, at-fault)
  • Specified perils / additional uninsured protections
⭐
EXAM FLAG: Even with optional coverages — no-fault SABs are statutory and always apply first from your own insurer.

05 — Third Party Liability & s.267.5 Threshold

Fault-based tort claims — the gate for pain & suffering
Third Party Liability
  • Minimum $200K required (standard: $1M+)
  • Fault must be proven
  • Economic damages: always available beyond SAB caps
  • Contributory negligence reduces damages proportionally
  • Pain & suffering: only if s.267.5 threshold met
s.267.5 — Pain & Suffering Gate

Must prove ONE of:

1
Death
2
Permanent serious disfigurement
3
Permanent serious impairment of an important function

"Important function" = walking · cognition · limb use · psychological function

Statutory Deductible
$47,913.01

Deducted from every pain & suffering award — even if you WIN.

$100,000 award → you receive $52,086.99

✓ CAN sue for pain & suffering
  • Permanent scarring / disfigurement
  • Permanent loss of limb function
  • Permanent cognitive impairment
  • Permanent psychological impairment
  • Death (estate/dependents)
✗ CANNOT sue for pain & suffering
  • Sprains/strains with full recovery
  • Whiplash with full recovery

  • Temporary soft tissue injury

06 — Coverage Trigger & Case Law

"Arising from use or operation" — Amos · Witlingham · Herbison
📖
The trigger phrase: "Arising directly or indirectly from the ownership, use OR operation of an automobile." Causation threshold is LOWEST for SABs — HIGHER for third party liability. See the Case Law tab for full analysis.

07 — UDAP: Unfair & Deceptive Acts or Practices

FSRA Rule 2021-001 — lower exam weight but included
DECEPTIVE (misleading information)
  • Intent irrelevant — inadvertent deception counts
  • Technically true statements can still be deceptive
  • Test: would a reasonable consumer be misled?
  • Total impression governs — not isolated wording
  • Silence/omission can be deceptive

The Coverage Trigger & Why It Matters

📖
The phrase: "Arising directly or indirectly from the ownership, use OR operation of an automobile" — triggers coverage under SABs, DCPD, and third party liability. Three SCC cases define how broadly (or narrowly) courts interpret it.
01
Amos v. Insurance Corporation of British Columbia (SCC)
Coverage type: Statutory Accident Benefits (No-Fault) · Carjacking
THE FACTS
  • Amos driving his van in California
  • Attacked by carjackers — shot while trying to drive away
  • Applied to ICBC for no-fault accident benefits
  • ICBC denied — lower courts: van was merely the "location" of a shooting
  • SCC granted appeal
THE AMOS TWO-PART TEST (SCC)
  • Part 1 — Purpose Test: Did the accident involve the ordinary activities to which automobiles are put?
  • Part 2 — Causal Link Test: Is there a meaningful nexus — more than merely incidental or fortuitous?
  • Does NOT require proximate causation
  • Vehicle does not need to be the instrument of injury
  • Statute interpreted broadly in favour of SAB coverage
✅ RESULT: Amos WON. Carjacking had real causal connection to vehicle use — attackers wanted the van. The van drew the attack. Not a random shooting.
02
Witlingham v. Insurer (SCC) — decided with Herbison
Coverage type: Underinsured Motorist (OPCF 44R) · Boulder dropped from overpass
THE FACTS
  • Witlinghams driving on I-95, North Carolina
  • Wrongdoers transported boulder by vehicle to an overpass, dropped it onto the Witlinghams' car
  • Catastrophic injuries — wrongdoers fled in same vehicle
  • Wrongdoers had only $25K USD coverage
  • Witlinghams claimed under their own OPCF 44R (Underinsured Motorist)
  • Court of Appeal found coverage — SCC reversed
SCC RULING — 3 CORE PRINCIPLES
  • Amos does NOT auto-extend to liability coverage
  • Tortious conduct must be tied to using the vehicle AS a vehicle
  • Dropping the boulder = intervening act severable from vehicle use
  • Transporting rocks ≠ continuation of vehicle use for the dropping
  • "But for" causation is NOT enough — need unbroken chain of causation
❌ RESULT: Coverage DENIED. The act of dropping the boulder was an intervening act severable from vehicle use. Causal chain was broken.
03
Herbison v. Lumbermens Mutual (SCC) — decided with Witlingham
Coverage type: Third Party Liability (s.239 Insurance Act) · Hunting accident
THE FACTS
  • Wolf being driven to a hunting site by his wife
  • Thought he saw a deer — told wife to stop the vehicle
  • Exited vehicle, took aim, fired — it was Herbison, not a deer
  • Wolf found negligent — Herbison awarded $832,000+ in damages
  • Herbison claimed under Wolf's auto insurer (s.239)
  • Court of Appeal agreed — SCC reversed
SCC RULING
  • "Directly or indirectly" relaxes causation but doesn't eliminate need for unbroken chain
  • Wolf's truck merely created opportunity in time and space
  • Exiting vehicle and shooting = independent intervening act
  • That act broke the chain between vehicle use and the injury
  • Driving to a location ≠ every subsequent act covered by auto insurance
❌ RESULT: Coverage DENIED. The shooting was an independent act that broke the causal chain. The truck created the opportunity — it didn't cause the injury.

HIGH PRIORITY — Know These Cold

HIGH
You ALWAYS claim SABs from your OWN insurer first — regardless of who caused the accident. Even as a pedestrian hit by another vehicle, you claim under your own auto policy.
HIGH
MIG does NOT equal s.267.5. MIG governs SAB (no-fault) entitlement only. s.267.5 governs whether you can sue for non-pecuniary tort damages. Similar in concept — legally distinct.
HIGH
Fault matters in THREE ways only: (1) Tort claims — fault must be proven. (2) Premium increases. (3) DCPD — only applies when NOT at fault. Fault does NOT affect SAB eligibility at all.
HIGH
The Amos two-part test: (1) Purpose — ordinary vehicle activity? (2) Causal link — meaningful nexus more than incidental? For SABs: broad interpretation, no proximate causation required. For liability coverage (Witlingham, Herbison): stronger unbroken causal chain required. Amos is still good law.

LIKELY TESTED — Understand Clearly

LIKELY
Know the 3 MIG exit conditions: permanent spinal impairment / significant loss of limb function / chronic disabling pain. Exiting MIG unlocks higher SAB limits AND opens non-economic tort claims.
LIKELY
The s.267.5 threshold — must prove ONE: Death | Permanent serious disfigurement | Permanent serious impairment of important function. The test is a legal classification — not how pain feels subjectively.
LIKELY
Economic damages always available in tort beyond SAB caps. s.267.5 only gates non-pecuniary claims. Lost income, out-of-pocket expenses, and future care costs require no threshold.
LIKELY
Witlingham and Herbison: both denied because the tortious act was an INTERVENING ACT severable from vehicle use. "But for" causation alone is not enough for liability coverage — need unbroken chain.

WATCH OUT — Common Traps

TRAP
IRBs are capped at 70% of gross income AND subject to weekly statutory caps. High earners are the most disadvantaged — you do not get full income replacement.
TRAP
Statutory deductible is $47,913.01. Even if you WIN a pain & suffering case and get $100K, the deductible comes off first — you receive $52,086.99.
TRAP
Uninsured automobile coverage is the FOURTH mandatory bucket — students often only remember SABs, DCPD, and TPL.
TRAP
UDAP: deception does NOT require intent. A technically true statement can still be deceptive if the overall impression misleads. Unfairness does not require deception at all.

7:06

PART 2 Coverage Trigger & Case Law — Amos, Witlingham, Herbison
pt2 — 3:52
So one of the key things that you have to understand about auto insurance in Ontario is how the Insurance Act shapes coverage obligations. So each section of OAP 1 has different language to trigger coverage depending on what the section governs. But in general, coverage is triggered under statutory accident benefits, liability and DCPD coverages where the loss arises directly or indirectly from the ownership, use or operation of an automobile or motor vehicle. It sounds similar. Sometimes it is. Others it isn't. And there has been a whole host of judicial ink spilled on the issue of what does 'directly or indirectly from the ownership, use or operation of an automobile' actually mean.
pt2 — 4:43
Some cases are easy — two cars collide, that you would expect to fall under your automobile insurance coverage, no problem. Other easy cases: a vehicle strikes a pedestrian, a vehicle strikes a cyclist, those are relatively easy cases where we see automobile insurance responding. But what about a case where a parent parks a vehicle, exits the vehicle with a child, and is hit by a car? Probably also on the easy spectrum, because it's exiting a vehicle struck by another vehicle. But it gets increasingly complicated, and that's what we're going to look at in these next three cases. They're all Supreme Court of Canada cases.
pt2 — 5:00 · AMOS v. ICBC
So the first one we'll look at is a case called Amos and the Insurance Corporation of British Columbia. Amos is now such a famous case, it's been adopted and applied in Ontario because the ICBC policy uses the same phrase of arising directly or indirectly from the ownership, use or operation of an automobile. Mr. Amos was driving his van when he was attacked, I believe in California, when he was attacked by several assailants and seriously injured by gunshots while he was attempting to drive away from them. So Mr. Amos was the victim of an attempted carjacking. He applied to his insurer, the ICBC, for statutory no fault accident benefits. ICBC denied benefits, and Amos sued for a declaration that he was entitled to cover. The British Columbia Supreme Court and the Court of Appeal both dismissed the action, holding that the injuries did not arise out of the use of the vehicle because the van was merely the situs of the shooting.
pt2 — 8:13 · The Amos Two-Part Test
So the Supreme Court rejected a rigid definition and instead articulated a two part test to determine whether an injury arises out of the ownership, use or operation of a vehicle. The first part of the test is the purpose test. And the question that you have to ask is, did the accident involve the ordinary and well known activities to which automobiles are put? I.e., was the vehicle engaged in a typical vehicular use at the time? And then the second part of the test is the causal link test: is there some nexus or causal relationship — not necessarily proximate or direct — between the injuries and the ownership, use and operation of the vehicle, that is one that is more than merely incidental or fortuitous? Crucially, the court emphasized that the second part does not require direct or proximate causation in the strict legal sense, but it does require a meaningful connection.
pt2 — 9:30 · Amos Result
So applying this test, the Supreme Court concluded the purpose test was easily satisfied. Mr. Amos was engaged in an ordinary vehicle activity — he was driving his own van down a street when the incident occurred. The causal link is the trickier portion of the analysis, but in this case, the Supreme Court found it satisfying. The shooting was not random or unrelated to the use of the van. The assailants' attempt to gain entry to the van was directly linked to Mr. Amos's ownership and use of the vehicle, which drew the attack. It was a carjacking. The Court explained that the vehicle does not have to be the instrument of the injury itself. It is enough that the injury has a real and non incidental connection to its use or ownership. The Supreme Court underscored that the statute must be interpreted broadly in favor of coverage because the legislative purpose of the mandatory no fault accident benefit scheme is to provide benefits for injuries connected to motor vehicle risks. The court rejected the conclusion that the van was merely the location of the attack. The attempt to enter the vehicle was triggered by Amos's use of it. Direct or proximate causation is not strictly required here, because we're in the statutory accident benefit language.
pt2 — 13:04 · Post-Amos Lower Courts
So post-Amos, you had a whole slew of lower court decisions that interpreted Amos in Ontario, and applied Amos very, very broadly and sometimes stretching the causal link to include injuries that really only had a remote or incidental connection to a vehicle. So some examples are: coverage was extended to include violent attacks or assaults near a parked vehicle, where courts sometimes assumed that the vehicle's presence at the site of the attack was enough to establish coverage. For example, an individual parks at a gas station to fill up their tank in the middle of winter, exits the vehicle, walks over to the pump, and while they're walking to the pump, they accidentally slip and fall on ice. And there were cases that said that was enough of a causal connection, because the vehicle got you to the place where you slipped and fell on ice. So there were a whole bunch of wonky decisions, and it led to Amos being heavily criticized as being an unwieldy test, or a test that yielded too much cover in too many situations.

So Amos needs some correction, and that eventually comes in two cases — a case called Witlingham and a case called Herbison. pt2 — 16:57 · WITLINGHAM — Facts
So let's look at the Witlingham case first. The respondents were Michael Chandra and Susanna Witlingham, and they were Ontario residents who were driving their vehicle on Interstate 95 in North Carolina when a large boulder was dropped from an overpass onto their car. The boulder was thrown by two individuals, Todd Farmer and Anthony Raynor, who had transported the boulder to the overpass by a motor vehicle and dropped it onto the highway below and then used that vehicle to flee the scene. As a result of the boulder striking the Witlinghams' car, Michael suffered catastrophic injuries, and his mother and sister suffered serious psychological harm. The wrongdoers were prosecuted and convicted, and their motor vehicle liability insurance had very low limits — US $25,000 — far less than the damages suffered by the Witlinghams. The Witlinghams had claimed inadequately insured motorist coverage under their policy's OPCF 44R Family Protection endorsement to recover civil damages the wrongdoers should have paid.
pt2 — 20:37 · WITLINGHAM — SCC Ruling
So the Supreme Court of Canada clarified that the Amos test does not automatically extend to third party indemnity coverage cases like this. The court emphasized that for liability cover to apply, the tortious conduct must be tied to using the vehicle as a vehicle and not some other purpose. The court held that the causal connection must be unbroken and it must be more than simply fortuitous. So this is a stronger causal connection test in the liability coverage than we see for statutory accident benefits. The Supreme Court emphasized three core principles. First, motorist coverage requires fault as a motorist — it's not enough that the wrongdoers used a vehicle at some point; the tort itself must be connected to driving the vehicle as a vehicle. Second, the contribution of the vehicle isn't enough if the tort is an intervening act. The act that caused the injuries — the dropping of the rock — was an intervening act that was not part of using the vehicle as a motor vehicle. The act of dropping the boulder was fully severable from the vehicle's use. And finally, 'directly or indirectly from the use or operation' does not collapse into a simple but-for test. The use or operation of the automobile must be linked by an unbroken chain of causation to the tortious act that caused the injury. So the court concluded that the tort — dropping the rocks — was separate from the use or operation of the vehicle, and because the causal chain was broken, the insurer wasn't liable.
pt2 — 23:50 · HERBISON — Facts & Ruling
The second case is the Herbison case — Herbison and Lumbermens Mutual. And the facts are, the action began after Mr. Herbison was injured by his hunting companion, Mr. Wolf. So Wolf was being driven to the hunting site by his wife at the time. While he's on the way to the spot where he's supposed to meet Mr. Herbison, he thinks he sees a deer in the woods. He tells his wife to stop the vehicle, gets out of the vehicle, takes aim, shoots. Fortunately, it was not a deer. It was, in fact, Mr. Herbison. Wolf was found to be negligent in shooting his gun, and Herbison was awarded $832,000 in damages plus interest and costs. Mr. Herbison sought to have Mr. Wolf's automobile insurer cover this judgment. The Ontario Court of Appeal reversed the trial judge, holding that the insurer must indemnify Mr. Herbison, because the truck contributed to the injury — because it was used to get to the hunting location. The Supreme Court allowed the appeal and reversed the Court of Appeal. The court said again — in third party liability coverage — there must be a causal connection beyond mere but-for causation. Wolf's truck did not cause Mr. Herbison's injury. It was the independent act of exiting the vehicle and shooting Mr. Herbison that broke that chain of causation between the use of the vehicle as a vehicle and the ultimate injury. Wolf's truck merely created the opportunity in time and space for the accident, but the injury didn't arise from using the truck as a vehicle.
pt2 — 27:30 · Poisoned Pizza Analogy & Key Takeaways
So the practical takeaway here is: driving to a location does not automatically make every subsequent act covered. A negligent act carried out off the highway unrelated to the vehicle as a vehicle will not attract liability coverage. The example I often like to use is: if you use a vehicle to deliver a poisoned pizza to someone on the other side of town, your automobile insurer got the pizza to the person, but the independent act of exiting the vehicle and committing that crime of intending to poison someone breaks the chain of causation. So you can't stretch these policies — they certainly have a loose causal relationship contained in them, but they have limits.
pt2 — 28:30 · Summary of Auto Insurance
Okay. So that brings us to the very end of the automobile insurance lecture. And key takeaways: Ontario's automobile insurance system is primarily a no fault system. You claim from your own insurer first for most losses. There are certain mandatory coverages that you have to purchase if you're a driver — third party liability, accident benefits, DCPD, uninsured automobile. DCPD, though, as of now, you can opt out. OAP 1 codifies these coverages and refers back to the Insurance Act. And Amos, Witlingham, and Herbison tell us how courts interpret the connection between accident and coverage triggers. And the causation requirement is going to be at its lowest for statutory accident benefits, and something more is required for third party liability coverages.

pt2 — 29:18
So that's the end of automobile insurance, and we're going to spend maybe another 10 minutes or so on unfair, deceptive acts or practices in Ontario insurance law. The Ontario Insurance Act is a Consumer Protection Act at its core, and we've already looked at lots of ways in which consumers are protected: statutory conditions, the misrepresentation, the cancellation, rescission rules that are set out in the act. Those are all consumer protection mechanisms. I'm only going to talk to you about one this afternoon. And that's the UDAP rule — Unfair Deceptive Practices rule. And it's a framework that's grounded in the Insurance Act. It's administered by the Financial Services Regulatory Authority of Ontario. We shorten that to FSRA. Under FSRA's governing statute, the governing instrument is FSRA Rule 2021-001, Unfair Deceptive Practices.
pt3 — 3:02
So the meaning of unfair or deceptive practice. All of this is built around making sure insurers do not engage in unfair or deceptive practices to the detriment of consumers. So what do unfair and deceptive mean? They are not rigidly or narrowly defined in any technical sense. Instead, they are broad and purposive standards, and FSRA has issued guidance to help insurers understand what they mean. Deceptive focuses on conduct that is misleading to the consumer, whether intentionally or not. And that's a key point — you don't have to show that an insurer intended to deceive. It will be enough if they inadvertently deceived.
pt3 — 3:47
Conduct is deceptive where it creates a false or misleading impression, misrepresents key features of a product or service, omits material information necessary to avoid misunderstanding. Critically, deception is assessed on the overall impression created, not the literal truth of individual statements. So this means a statement can be technically accurate and still be deceptive. Silence or omission can be deceptive if it distorts understanding. The test is effectively: would a reasonable consumer be misled in the circumstances?
pt3 — 4:29
So unfair is similar, but it's broader and focuses on consumer outcomes and treatment, not just information. Conduct is unfair where it causes consumer harm that is not reasonably justified, exploits consumer vulnerability, undermines the consumer's ability to make informed decisions, and results in outcomes that are inequitable or inconsistent with reasonable expectations. Importantly, unfairness does not require deception. A practice can be fully transparent and accurately disclosed and yet still be unfair in its effect. So deceptive is misleading the consumer. Unfair is treating the consumer inequitably or producing unjust outcomes. Most real world UDAP involves overlap, but analytically, deception is about information integrity, and unfairness is about substantive treatment and outcomes.
pt3 — 5:20
Any act or omission that is unfair or deceptive to a consumer is prohibited, and it applies to acts and omissions. It applies to the entire insurance life cycle, and is effects-based, not intent-based. The UDAP rule and FSRA guidance identify four main categories. First, misleading representations — deceptive conduct. These include false statements, half-truths, ambiguous or exaggerated claims, marketing that creates a misleading overall impression. And the key principle is the total impression governs, not isolated wording. Second bucket is material omissions — deceptive conduct. This includes failure to disclose key limitations, withholding information necessary for informed decisions, and structuring disclosures in a way that obscures important facts.
pt3 — 6:22
The third bucket is unfair claims practices — and this is both unfair and deceptive — and includes unreasonable delays in claims handling, inadequate investigation, poor or unclear communication, outcomes that do not reflect a fair assessment.
