01 — Ontario's Hybrid Auto Insurance System

The foundation — understand this before anything else
🔵 No-Fault Side
  • Statutory Accident Benefits (SABs)
  • Claim from YOUR OWN insurer first
  • Fault is completely irrelevant
  • Designed for speed and certainty
  • Every injured person is compensated
🔴 Tort Side
  • Third Party Liability claims
  • Fault must be established
  • Sue for economic + pain & suffering damages
  • Contributory negligence applies
  • Governed by s.267.5 threshold
📌
Key framing: Ontario leans toward no-fault (more than most provinces, less than Quebec). All mandatory coverages are set by legislation — insurers cannot change the basic package under OAP 1.

02 — Statutory Accident Benefits (SABs)

No-fault benefits — respond first, regardless of who caused the accident
Eligibility Trigger
  • Injury from use or operation of a motor vehicle
  • Covers: driver · passenger · pedestrian · cyclist
  • Claim from own insurer first — fault irrelevant
Priority Rules — Who Pays?
1
Your own insurer
2
Spouse's insurer
3
Insurer of vehicle you were in
4
MVACF (last resort)
SAB Benefit Types
  • Medical / Rehab / Attendant Care
  • IRBs — 70% of gross income, weekly caps
  • Non-Earner Benefits (students/unemployed)
  • Caregiver Benefits
  • Death & Funeral Benefits
  • Optional: housekeeping, educational expenses
⚠️
Repeat emphasis from lecture (said 3 times): Fault does NOT matter for SABs. You receive benefits even if you caused the accident. This is the defining feature of no-fault benefits.

03 — Minor Injury Guideline (MIG)

O. Reg 34/10, s.3(1) — caps SAB treatment for soft tissue injuries
Injuries Within MIG
  • Sprains · Strains · Whiplash
  • Contusions · Abrasions · Lacerations
  • Sub-luxations
  • Full recovery typically expected
  • Strict cap on treatment funding
3 Ways to EXIT the MIG
1
Permanent spinal impairment
2
Significant loss of limb function
3
Chronic disabling pain
What Exiting MIG Unlocks
  • Higher SAB limits (non-catastrophic → higher caps)
  • Catastrophic = very high limits + lifetime care
  • Access to non-economic tort damages (pain & suffering)
  • MIG classification can change as injury evolves
🚨
Most common student error: MIG ONLY governs SAB (no-fault) benefit entitlement. It does NOT apply to tort-based claims. MIG ≠ s.267.5. They are similar concepts but legally distinct.

04 — DCPD & Uninsured Automobile Coverage

Two more mandatory buckets under OAP 1
DCPD — Direct Compensation Property Damage

Your insurer pays your vehicle damage when you're NOT at fault. All 4 must apply:

  • Accident occurred in Ontario
  • At least one other vehicle involved
  • Other vehicle insured by licensed Ontario insurer
  • You are not at fault under Fault Determination Rules
  • Your insurer then subrogate against the at-fault party
  • As of Jan 1, 2024 — drivers may opt out by signing a form
  • At-fault drivers: no DCPD — need optional collision cover
Uninsured Automobile Coverage (4th Mandatory Bucket)
  • Covers injury when at-fault driver has no insurance
  • Covers hit-and-run situations (unidentified driver)
  • Your own insurer steps in to provide coverage
Optional Add-Ons (not mandatory)
  • Enhanced accident benefits (higher limits)
  • Collision & Comprehensive (own vehicle, at-fault)
  • Specified perils / additional uninsured protections
⭐
EXAM FLAG: Even with optional coverages — no-fault SABs are statutory and always apply first from your own insurer. Optional coverage supplements but never replaces the mandatory no-fault layer.

05 — Third Party Liability & s.267.5 Threshold

Fault-based tort claims — the gate for pain & suffering
Third Party Liability
  • Minimum $200K required (standard: $1M+)
  • Fault must be proven
  • Economic damages: always available beyond SAB caps
  • Contributory negligence reduces damages proportionally
  • Pain & suffering: only if s.267.5 threshold met
s.267.5 — Pain & Suffering Gate

Must prove ONE of:

1
Death
2
Permanent serious disfigurement
3
Permanent serious impairment of an important function

"Important function" = walking · cognition · limb use · psychological function

Statutory Deductible
$47,913.01

Deducted from every pain & suffering award — even if you WIN.

$100,000 award → you receive $52,086.99

Purpose: reduce small claims, control premiums, encourage settlements.

✓ CAN sue for pain & suffering
  • Permanent scarring / disfigurement
  • Permanent loss of limb function
  • Permanent cognitive impairment
  • Permanent psychological impairment
  • Death (estate/dependents)
✗ CANNOT sue for pain & suffering
  • Sprains/strains with full recovery
  • Whiplash with full recovery
  • Minor psychological distress
  • Short-term pain, no lasting impairment
  • Temporary soft tissue injury
📋
Court standard: Impairment must be real and substantial, interfere meaningfully with normal life activities, and be supported by expert medical evidence. Subjective complaints alone are insufficient.

06 — Coverage Trigger: "Arising from Use or Operation"

The phrase that runs through all OAP 1 coverages — and the source of all the case law
📖
The trigger phrase: "Arising directly or indirectly from the ownership, use OR operation of an automobile" — appears across SABs, DCPD, and third party liability. Courts have wrestled with what this means in non-obvious cases.
Easy Cases (obvious coverage)
  • Two cars collide
  • Vehicle strikes a pedestrian
  • Vehicle strikes a cyclist
  • Exiting a vehicle, struck by another car
Hard Cases (the real exam material)
  • Carjacking — is the vehicle the cause? (Amos)
  • Boulder dropped from overpass (Witlingham)
  • Hunting accident after driving to site (Herbison)
  • Slip & fall at gas station after parking
⚖️
Critical principle: The causation threshold is at its LOWEST for SABs (statutory accident benefits) and HIGHER for third party liability coverages. Same phrase — different standard depending on what type of coverage is in play.

07 — UDAP: Unfair & Deceptive Acts or Practices

FSRA Rule 2021-001 — consumer protection in Ontario insurance (lower exam weight but included)
DECEPTIVE (misleading information)
  • Intent is irrelevant — inadvertent deception counts
  • Creates false or misleading impression
  • Misrepresents key product features
  • Omits material information
  • Technically true statements can still be deceptive
  • Test: would a reasonable consumer be misled?
  • Silence/omission can be deceptive
UNFAIR (inequitable outcomes)
  • Broader than deception — focuses on outcomes
  • Causes consumer harm not reasonably justified
  • Exploits consumer vulnerability
  • Undermines informed decision-making
  • A practice can be fully disclosed yet still UNFAIR
  • No deception required — unfairness is about treatment
01 — Misleading Representations
  • False statements, half-truths
  • Ambiguous/exaggerated claims
  • Marketing creating misleading overall impression
  • Total impression governs — not isolated wording
02 — Material Omissions
  • Failure to disclose key limitations
  • Withholding information for informed decisions
  • Structuring disclosures to obscure facts
03 — Unfair Claims Practices
  • Unreasonable delays in claims handling
  • Inadequate investigation
  • Poor or unclear communication
  • Outcomes not reflecting fair assessment
04 — Unfair Sales & Distribution
  • High-pressure sales tactics
  • Exploiting consumer vulnerability
  • Tied selling (buy X to get Y)
  • Practices impairing informed choice
🏛️
Enforcement: Administered by FSRA. Can impose monetary penalties, require conduct corrections, mandate new policies. Findings can be published (reputational risk). Can also support class action remedies — but UDAP findings themselves do not create a direct civil cause of action.

QUESTION — TAP TO REVEAL

The Coverage Trigger & Why It Matters

📖
The phrase: "Arising directly or indirectly from the ownership, use OR operation of an automobile" — triggers coverage under SABs, DCPD, and third party liability. Three SCC cases define exactly how broadly (or narrowly) courts interpret it.
01
Amos v. Insurance Corporation of British Columbia (SCC)
Coverage type: Statutory Accident Benefits (No-Fault) · Carjacking
THE FACTS
  • Amos was driving his van in California
  • Attacked by carjackers — shot while trying to drive away
  • Applied to ICBC for no-fault accident benefits
  • ICBC denied — lower courts agreed: van was merely the "location" of the shooting
  • SCC granted appeal
THE AMOS TWO-PART TEST (SCC)
  • Part 1 — Purpose Test: Did the accident involve the ordinary activities to which automobiles are put?
  • Part 2 — Causal Link Test: Is there a meaningful nexus between the injury and ownership/use of the vehicle — more than merely incidental or fortuitous?
  • Does NOT require direct or proximate causation
  • Vehicle does not need to be the instrument of injury
  • Statute interpreted broadly in favour of SAB coverage
✅ RESULT: Amos WON. Carjacking had real causal connection to vehicle use — attackers wanted the van. The van drew the attack. Shooting arose from use and ownership of the vehicle.
02
Witlingham v. Insurer (SCC) — decided with Herbison
Coverage type: Underinsured Motorist (OPCF 44R — Family Protection) · Boulder dropped from overpass
THE FACTS
  • Witlinghams driving on I-95, North Carolina
  • Wrongdoers (Farmer & Raynor) transported a boulder by vehicle to an overpass
  • Dropped the boulder onto the Witlinghams' car — catastrophic injuries
  • Wrongdoers fled in the same vehicle
  • Wrongdoers had only $25K USD in coverage — far below damages
  • Witlinghams claimed under their own OPCF 44R (Underinsured Motorist)
  • Court of Appeal found coverage — SCC reversed
SCC RULING — 3 CORE PRINCIPLES
  • Amos does NOT auto-extend to 3rd party liability coverage — different standard applies
  • Must be fault as a motorist: tortious conduct must be tied to using the vehicle AS a vehicle — not some other purpose
  • Intervening act: Dropping the boulder was separate from driving — fully severable from vehicle use
  • Transporting rocks ≠ continuation of vehicle use for the dropping
  • "But for" causation is NOT enough — need unbroken chain of causation
  • Vehicle's role cannot be merely incidental
❌ RESULT: Coverage DENIED. The act of dropping the boulder was an intervening act severable from vehicle use. The causal chain was broken.
03
Herbison v. Lumbermens Mutual (SCC) — decided with Witlingham
Coverage type: Third Party Liability (s.239 Insurance Act) · Hunting accident
THE FACTS
  • Wolf was being driven to a hunting site by his wife
  • Thought he saw a deer — told wife to stop the vehicle
  • Exited the vehicle, took aim, fired — it was Herbison, not a deer
  • Wolf found negligent — Herbison awarded $832,000+ in damages
  • Herbison argued Wolf's auto insurer should cover under s.239
  • Court of Appeal agreed — SCC reversed
SCC RULING
  • "Directly or indirectly" relaxes the causation requirement — but does NOT eliminate the need for an unbroken chain
  • Wolf's truck merely created the opportunity in time and space for the accident
  • Exiting the vehicle and shooting was an independent intervening act
  • That independent act broke the chain between vehicle use and the injury
  • Driving to a location does NOT make every subsequent act covered by auto insurance
  • "But for" causation alone is insufficient
❌ RESULT: Coverage DENIED. The shooting was an independent act that broke the causal chain. The truck created the opportunity — it did not cause the injury.

🔑
The key takeaway: Amos is still good law — Witlingham and Herbison did not overturn it. They corrected the over-extension of Amos by lower courts. The Amos test (purpose + causal link) applies to SABs with a low causal threshold. For liability coverage, a stronger unbroken chain is required. Driving to a location does NOT make every subsequent act covered.
🍕
Professor's poisoned pizza analogy: If you use a vehicle to deliver a poisoned pizza across town, your automobile insurer does not cover the poisoning. The vehicle got the pizza there — but the independent criminal act of poisoning breaks the causal chain. The policies have limits.

HIGH PRIORITY — Know These Cold

HIGH
You ALWAYS claim SABs from your OWN insurer first — regardless of who caused the accident. Even as a pedestrian hit by another vehicle, you claim under your own auto policy. This trips students up constantly.
HIGH
MIG does NOT equal s.267.5. MIG governs SAB (no-fault) entitlement only. s.267.5 governs whether you can sue for non-pecuniary (pain & suffering) tort damages. They are similar in concept but legally distinct and apply in completely different contexts.
HIGH
Fault matters in ONLY THREE ways: (1) Tort claims — fault must be proven to sue. (2) Premium increases — at-fault history raises your premiums. (3) DCPD — only applies when you are NOT at fault. Fault does NOT affect SAB eligibility at all.
HIGH
The Amos two-part test: (1) Purpose — was it an ordinary vehicle activity? (2) Causal link — meaningful nexus more than incidental? For SABs, this is interpreted broadly. For liability coverage (Witlingham, Herbison), a stronger unbroken causal chain is required. Amos is still good law.

LIKELY TESTED — Understand Clearly

LIKELY
Know the 3 MIG exit conditions cold: permanent spinal impairment / significant loss of limb function / chronic disabling pain. Exiting MIG unlocks higher SAB limits AND opens the door to non-economic tort claims.
LIKELY
The s.267.5 threshold — must prove ONE of: Death | Permanent serious disfigurement | Permanent serious impairment of an important function (walking, cognition, limb use, psychological function). The test is a legal classification — not how the pain feels subjectively.
LIKELY
Economic damages (lost income beyond SABs, out-of-pocket, future care costs) are ALWAYS available via tort — the s.267.5 threshold does NOT gate economic claims. Only non-pecuniary (pain & suffering) damages require meeting the threshold.
LIKELY
DCPD 4 conditions: Ontario accident + another Ontario-insured vehicle involved + you are not at fault. As of Jan 1, 2024, drivers can opt out. At-fault drivers get NO DCPD — they need optional collision/comprehensive coverage for their own vehicle damage.
LIKELY
Witlingham and Herbison: both coverage denied because the tortious act was an INTERVENING ACT severable from vehicle use. The vehicle transported people/things to the scene — but the injury-causing act (dropping boulder / shooting) broke the causal chain. "But for" causation alone is not enough for liability coverage.

WATCH OUT — Common Traps

TRAP
IRBs are capped at 70% of gross income AND subject to weekly statutory caps. High earners are the most disadvantaged. You do NOT get full income replacement — the caps bite harder the more you earn.
TRAP
Statutory deductible is $47,913.01. Even if you WIN a pain & suffering case and get a $100K award, the deductible comes off first — you receive $52,086.99. The deductible is meant to discourage small-to-medium claims and encourage settlement.
TRAP
Contributory negligence in Ontario tort = damages reduced proportionally to plaintiff's own fault. Ontario does NOT use all-or-nothing contributory negligence. Partial fault = partial recovery, not zero recovery.
TRAP
Uninsured automobile coverage is the FOURTH mandatory bucket — students often only remember SABs, DCPD, and TPL. It covers hit-and-run and uninsured drivers by having your own insurer step in.
TRAP
UDAP: deception does NOT require intent — inadvertent deception counts. A technically true statement can still be deceptive if it creates a misleading overall impression. Unfairness does not require deception at all — a fully disclosed practice can still be unfair in its effects.
